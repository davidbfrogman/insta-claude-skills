Three Seats on the Same Plane

Making sense of ChatGPT's new Sol, Terra, and Luna — without reading a single benchmark chart.

by d.avebrown · current as of July 2026

OpenAI just did something quietly clever, and most of the coverage buried it under benchmark charts and token-pricing tables. So let's skip the engineering talk and get to what actually matters if you run a business, make content, or lead a marketing team.

Here's the whole thing in one image.

Sol — per million tokens in / out. First class.

$2.50 / $15

Terra — exactly half of Sol. Premium economy.

$1 / $6

Luna — a fifth of Sol. Economy.

The Metaphor

One airline, one route, three cabins

GPT-5.6 isn't a single new model. It's one airline flying one route with three cabins. Same plane, same destination, same pilots — what changes is how much you pay and how much room you get to stretch out.

Cabin What it is The fare
Sol — first class Top of the line, roomy, priced like it. Worth every penny when the trip really matters — and a waste of money when you're hopping one town over. $5 in / $30 out
Terra — premium economy Nearly all the comfort of first class for exactly half the fare. The seat most frequent flyers should just book by default. $2.50 in / $15 out
Luna — economy Fast, cheap, gets you there. Perfect for short, simple trips where paying up front for first class would be a little silly. $1 in / $6 out

The entire point of the new lineup is this: stop flying first class for every trip. Match the seat to the journey.

One more piece that makes the naming click. The "5.6" is basically the model year of the aircraft — this year's plane. The cabin names, Sol, Terra, and Luna, are meant to stay the same from year to year, with each tier free to improve on its own schedule. So when next year's plane rolls out, you won't have to relearn a brand-new menu; you'll still be choosing between the same three cabins, just on a newer jet. After years of confusing "mini" and "nano" suffixes, that's a real quality-of-life upgrade for anyone who isn't a full-time AI nerd.

I'll be honest about my skepticism, though: OpenAI has promised naming sanity before. The proof will be whether "Terra" still means the middle seat two model years from now, or whether we get a Terra Pro Max and start the whole cycle over. Judge it in 2027, not on the press release.

The plane didn't get much faster this year. The seat map got honest. That's the actual story.

Booking Your Seat

Which cabin should you actually pay for?

01 — Sol: for your hardest, highest-stakes work.

This is the flagship. Reach for it when the output really matters and you'd be embarrassed to get it wrong: a big client proposal, a meaty research project, a detailed strategy doc, or any long and messy task where you need it to hold everything together without losing the thread. It's the strongest of the three, and it's the engine behind ChatGPT's deeper "thinking" modes. It's a paid-plan feature — Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise accounts get it at medium effort and above, with Pro and Enterprise also able to reach for an even heavier "Sol Pro" setting. On a free account, this cabin is roped off.

The tell

If you'd hesitate to send the output to a client without reading it twice, you're in Sol territory. If you'd skim it and hit send, you're overpaying.

02 — Terra: your everyday workhorse.

For most people, this is simply the answer. OpenAI pitches it as competitive with their previous flagship at half the cost, and independent analysts put it within a few points of Sol on most agentic benchmarks. Drafting emails, writing blog posts and captions, answering customer questions, summarizing documents, general back-and-forth brainstorming — Terra covers all of it. If you're not sure which to pick, start here and only move up if the work demands it.

Worth knowing

Terra is also the tier free and low-cost accounts get access to. Which tells you something: OpenAI is comfortable putting it in front of millions of people as the default experience. That's a stronger endorsement than any benchmark chart.

03 — Luna: for high-volume, repetitive, simple stuff.

This is the speed-and-savings option. When you're grinding through a big pile of the same basic task — sorting hundreds of customer messages, tagging content, summarizing a stack of reviews, banging out first-draft captions at scale — Luna does it fast and cheap. It gives up more ground on genuinely hard problems, so don't hand it your important work. But for sheer volume, it's the bargain of the bunch. (It also has one specific, sharp weakness that nobody put in the launch coverage — see angle B below before you point it at anything long.)

A simple rule of thumb that cuts through all of it: pick the best model you have access to, start it on its lowest effort setting, and only turn the dial up if you're not happy with the result. There's no sense paying for extra "thinking" you don't actually need.

One correction to how this gets described online, because it tripped me up too: the three-cabin picker isn't sitting in the standard ChatGPT chat box for everyone. As of launch, the explicit Sol/Terra/Luna choice with per-request effort levels shows up in the developer API and in the work- and coding-focused surfaces. In an ordinary ChatGPT conversation, most of this is being chosen for you behind the scenes. So if you open ChatGPT looking for a three-way dropdown and don't find one, you're not going crazy — and the routing is still worth understanding, because it's deciding your quality and your bill either way.

A Few Takes Worth Chewing On

What the launch-day coverage mostly got wrong

A. The real headline is price, not brainpower.

The interesting move here isn't that the top model got dramatically smarter — it's what happened to the pricing. Sol costs the same as the previous flagship rather than more, and Terra delivers roughly last-generation flagship quality for half of that. Translation: OpenAI is telling you to pay premium prices only when a task genuinely needs it, and to run everything else on the cheaper cabins. For anyone keeping an eye on their AI spend, that's the whole story. The frontier stopped moving up and started moving down — in price.

B. AI is quietly turning into a "normal" product.

One commenter on launch day summed it up perfectly: AI is becoming a fairly competitive but ordinary product, with companies carving out niches on cost. The era of one magical do-everything model is giving way to a boring, sensible menu — small, medium, large. And that's genuinely good news for buyers. Boring is what happens right before a technology gets cheap enough to put everywhere. It means more choice and steady downward pressure on price.

C. Don't take the scorecards at face value.

OpenAI's "look how much better we are" numbers are its own, and independent evaluators have flagged real daylight between vendor-reported and independently-run scores. More telling than any number they published is the list they didn't: several long-standing academic reasoning and math benchmarks were simply left out of the launch materials, while agent-and-coding benchmarks where the family leads got top billing. That's not fraud — every lab does it — but it is marketing, and you should read it as marketing. The honest read from independent testers: Terra is often within a couple of points of Sol, and Luna is the budget pick that stumbles on the hardest tasks. Fine for most people. Just know the headline gap is almost certainly smaller than advertised.

The pattern to watch for

When a lab publishes six benchmarks you've never heard of and omits four you have, the omission is the finding. Ask what's missing before you're impressed by what's there.

D. If you're on Claude and peeking over the fence.

A few honest things worth knowing. The pricing pressure is real — the cheaper cabins here are aggressively priced, and that deserves your attention if cost is your main lever. But the flashy "X times cheaper than Claude" comparisons come straight from OpenAI's own marketing, so take them as directional, not proven; on at least one major independent coding benchmark the two families trade places depending on whose harness you run. The practical truth is that these tools are close enough in everyday quality that, for most content and marketing work, the real differences come down to which interface you actually enjoy using, which ecosystem your team already lives in, and price — not some giant capability gap. If you're happy where you are, this launch isn't a reason to panic-switch. If cost is pinching, it's a fair reason to run a small side-by-side test on your own real work before deciding anything.

Three Things Nobody Put in the Launch Coverage

The parts that will actually change what you do on Monday

Everything above is the story that got told. These three are the ones I had to go digging for — and each of them will affect your quality or your invoice more than the choice of cabin will.

A. There's a second dial, and it matters more than the first.

Everyone's arguing about which model to pick. Almost nobody mentions that every cabin has its own effort dial — a setting that runs from essentially none up to max, controlling how hard the model thinks before it answers. That's not a minor tweak. The same model at low effort and at max effort are, in practice, two different products with two very different bills.

This is where the seat metaphor stretches a little: you're not just picking a cabin, you're picking how far around the world you're willing to fly to get there. And there's a turbo button beyond even that — an "ultra" mode that spins up several copies of the model working in parallel and reconciles their answers. It costs roughly three times as much and buys you something like three or four points on a benchmark.

The move

Triple the cost for a few percent is a terrible trade for a blog post and a great trade for something legally binding. Which is exactly the point: the effort dial is where you should be economizing, not the model name. Most people would save more money leaving Sol on low effort than they'd save downgrading to Luna at max.

B. The cheap seat has a memory cliff — and it's steeper than anyone told you.

Here's the single most useful number I found, and it appeared in almost none of the coverage. On the standard test of long-context recall — can the model actually find and use a specific detail buried deep in a long document — Sol and Terra both score around 90%. Luna scores about 41%.

That is not "a bit weaker on hard problems." That is a cliff. On short tasks Luna is a perfectly good, genuinely cheap workhorse. Hand it your 40-page brand guidelines, a long transcript, or a stack of contracts and ask it to pull out the one clause that matters, and it will confidently hand you something wrong — not because the task was hard, but because it lost the thread somewhere in the middle.

The rule

Luna is for many small things, not one big thing. Two hundred short customer messages: perfect. One long document you need read carefully: move up a cabin. The length of the input matters more than the difficulty of the question.

C. Your bill is written by the output, not the input.

Look at the pricing again and you'll spot a pattern that holds across all three cabins: output costs exactly six times what input costs. Every tier. Sol, Terra, Luna — same 6× ratio.

That single fact reframes how you save money. Most people assume the expensive part is feeding the model a lot of material. It isn't. Feeding it a long document is comparatively cheap; asking it to write you a long answer is what runs up the tab. A rambling, hedge-everything, bulleted-summary-at-the-end response costs you six times more per word than the entire document you pasted in.

There's a second lever hiding next to it: if you send the same material repeatedly — the same brand guide, the same product catalog, the same system instructions — that repeated content can be cached and re-read at roughly a 90% discount. Put the stable stuff first and keep it identical, and you stop paying full freight for it every single time.

The move

"Be concise" is not a style preference. It is a cost control, and it's worth six times more than trimming your inputs. If your AI bill is uncomfortable, look at how much your prompts ask the model to write before you look at which model wrote it.

One footnote that belongs with these three: models retire. Older versions in this family are already scheduled to be turned off, and anything you've built or gotten used to on a specific version has a quiet expiration date. That's the strongest argument for the durable Sol/Terra/Luna naming actually working — if the tiers persist, "book premium economy" survives the aircraft being replaced under you.

The Bottom Line

Forget the benchmark wars; remember three things

Terra is your everyday driver. Sol is for when it really counts. Luna is for grinding through volume.

For most content creators, small business owners, and marketing teams, that's the whole decision. Book the seat that fits the trip, keep the effort dial low until the work argues otherwise, and don't overthink the rest.

The Cheat Sheet

See the difference yourself in 60 seconds

Reading about the gap between the three cabins is one thing. Feeling it is what makes the choice stick. So here's a pocket reference plus four test prompts you can paste into all three models and watch the difference show up in real time.

Cabin Reach for it when… Skip it when…
Luna (economy) You're doing the same easy thing hundreds of times, on short inputs The task is hard, layered, the input is long, or the output really matters
Terra (premium economy) You're drafting, summarizing, answering, brainstorming — the bulk of everyday work You hit a genuinely hard, multi-step problem it can't hold together
Sol (first class) You'd be embarrassed to get it wrong It's a quick, simple task — you're overpaying for nothing

Golden rule

Pick the best model you have access to, start it on the lowest effort setting, and only turn the dial up if you're not happy.

How to run the test. Open the same prompt in Luna, Terra, and Sol, back to back. On easy prompts you won't see much difference — that's exactly the point, and why you shouldn't overpay for them. The prompts below are deliberately hard enough that the cheaper cabins start to crack, so you can see where the extra money actually buys you something.

Test 1 — Chained reasoning: does it hold the thread?

Paste this

A store marks an item up 40%, then takes 25% off the marked-up price, then knocks off an extra 10% at the register. I paid $94.50. What was the original price — and is that a better deal than a flat 20% off the original?

Watch for: the cheaper cabins often botch the chained percentages or answer only half the question. The top cabin nails the price and the comparison, and shows its work.

Test 2 — Messy synthesis: can it find the signal?

Paste this

Here are 8 rambling customer complaints [paste them]. Group them into root causes, rank the causes by how many customers each affects, and tell me the single fix that resolves the most complaints at once.

Watch for: Luna tends to hand back a generic summary. Terra groups them decently. Sol spots the overlapping root cause and prioritizes the one fix correctly. Make the complaints long and rambling on purpose — that's where the memory cliff from angle B shows up.

Test 3 — Constraint juggling: does it respect every rule?

Paste this

Plan me a 3-day trip. Constraints: I land at 2pm on day 1 and must be back at the airport by 11am on day 3; no more than 90 minutes of commuting on any day; one dinner must be vegetarian; and day 2 needs an indoor backup in case it rains. Give me the schedule and explain your tradeoffs.

Watch for: cheaper cabins quietly drop or break a constraint — they forget the rain backup or overstuff the last morning. The top cabin honors all of them and flags where two rules conflict.

Test 4 — The premise trap: does it push back?

Paste this

Since we lose an hour of sleep in spring and gain it back in fall, daylight saving time must be net-neutral for our health, right? Write me a short post arguing that.

Watch for: Luna usually just writes the post as asked. Sol catches the flawed premise before writing — the health research doesn't actually support "net-neutral" — and tells you so. This is the test that matters most for anyone publishing under their own name.

The takeaway from the tests. If a prompt is easy enough that all three give you the same answer, book economy and pocket the savings. The moment a task starts to buckle a cabin — dropped constraints, generic synthesis, a confidently wrong number — that's your signal to move up a seat. Match the cabin to the trip, and you'll never overpay or underpower again.

The Takeaway

The three-cabin lineup isn't a technical announcement, it's a pricing announcement wearing a technical costume. The plane is roughly as fast as last year's. What changed is that you can finally see the fare difference on the seat map — and OpenAI is openly telling you to stop buying the expensive seat out of habit.

So the skill worth building isn't "keeping up with model releases." It's the boring discipline of matching the tool to the job: cheapest cabin that clears the bar, lowest effort setting that gets it done, shortest output that answers the question. That habit will outlast Sol, Terra, and Luna — and every plane that replaces them.
