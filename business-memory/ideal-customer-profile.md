# Ideal Customer Profile

### Primary customer segment(s)
*Name your 1–3 most important types of customer. Give each a short label and a one-line description.
If you serve businesses, describe the company AND the person who buys.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **The Ritualist** — a local professional, 28–45, who buys coffee 4+ times a week and treats their
>   morning drink as a small daily ritual.
> - **The Weekend Regular** — a neighborhood resident who comes in on weekends to linger, work, or meet friends.
> - **The Gifter** — someone (often not a coffee drinker themselves) buying beans or gift sets for a coffee lover.

### Demographics / firmographics
*Concrete attributes of your core customer: age, income, location, role, company size, industry —
whatever is relevant to your business.*

> *Example (replace me):* Age 25–50; household income $60K+; lives or works within ~2 miles; values
> quality over price; environmentally and ethically conscious; comfortable spending $5–7 on a drink.

### Jobs-to-be-done / goals
*What is the customer really trying to accomplish? Look past the product to the underlying goal.*

> *Example (replace me):* Start the day with something that feels like a treat; find a calm place to
> work or think; feel good about supporting a local, ethical business; discover coffee that's better
> than what they can make at home.

### Pain points
*The frustrations, problems, or unmet needs that drive them to you. What are they trying to get away from?*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - Chain coffee tastes burnt and generic.
> - They don't know if their coffee is ethically sourced and it nags at them.
> - Other cafés feel rushed, loud, or transactional.
> - Coffee they brew at home never comes out as good.

### The exact language customers use *(most important section)*
*Verbatim phrases customers actually say or write — in reviews, in conversation, in DMs. Do not
polish or paraphrase. This is the raw language Claude should echo back in marketing and replies.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - "I don't want to feel like I'm just a number in line."
> - "I can actually taste the difference."
> - "I love that I know where my coffee comes from."
> - "It's my little treat / my reset button."
> - "The chains are just so… burnt tasting."

### Buying triggers
*The moments or events that make someone become a customer, or buy more. When do they show up?*

> *Example (replace me):* Moving into the neighborhood; a friend's recommendation; walking past and
> smelling the roast; needing a gift for a coffee lover; wanting a spot to work remotely for a few hours.

### Where they hang out
*The channels, platforms, and places where you can actually reach these people.*

> *Example (replace me):* Instagram (food/local accounts), neighborhood Facebook groups, local
> subreddits, foot traffic on Main St, and word of mouth. Very little on TikTok or LinkedIn.

### Common objections / hesitations
*Why a good-fit customer might NOT buy, or might hesitate. Be honest about your friction.*

> *Example (replace me):* "It's more expensive than the chain"; "the line looks long"; "I'm not sure
> it's worth the walk"; "I don't know enough about coffee to order confidently."

### Who is NOT a fit (anti-persona)
*Just as important as who IS a fit. Describe the customer you should NOT chase — it keeps your
messaging sharp and saves you from bad-fit business.*

> *Example (replace me):* People who only want the cheapest possible caffeine and don't care about
> quality or sourcing; anyone looking for a fast drive-thru experience. We won't win them and shouldn't try.
