# Product & Positioning

### Product / service catalog
*Your offerings. For each: what it is, who it's for, and your price posture (budget / mid / premium).
Keep it to your main lines — you don't need every SKU.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **Espresso & pour-over drinks** — for daily café customers. Premium ($5–7).
> - **Whole-bean coffee (12 oz bags)** — for at-home brewers. Premium ($18–22/bag).
> - **Bean subscription** — for loyal regulars who want fresh coffee shipped monthly. Mid-premium ($20/mo).
> - **Gift sets** — for gifters. Premium ($35–60).
> - **Wholesale beans** — for local restaurants. Volume pricing.

### How we describe the product (plain language)
*Explain your core offering the way you'd tell a friend — no jargon. This is the "elevator" description
Claude will reuse.*

> *Example (replace me):* We roast small batches of coffee ourselves every week, so what you drink is
> always fresh, and we can tell you exactly which farm it came from. It's the kind of coffee that makes
> you slow down for a second.

### Core value propositions
*The 2–4 concrete benefits customers get. Benefits, not features — "roasted weekly" is a feature;
"never stale" is the benefit.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - Always fresh (never more than 7 days off the roaster).
> - Coffee you can feel good about (direct-trade, ethically sourced).
> - A genuinely warm place to be, not a rushed transaction.
> - Approachable expertise — we help you find what you'll love.

### Positioning statement
*Fill in this template. It forces you to name your customer, their need, your category, your benefit,
and your key alternative. This is the single most important line in the file.*

> **For** [target customer] **who** [need/problem], **we are the** [category] **that** [key benefit],
> **unlike** [main alternative], **because** [reason to believe].

> *Example (replace me):* For neighborhood coffee lovers who want a daily cup they can feel good about,
> Ember & Oak is the local roaster-café that serves ethically sourced coffee roasted fresh each week,
> unlike the chain down the street, because we roast on-site and know every farm we buy from.

### Competitive landscape & how we differ
*Your main alternatives (direct competitors AND "do nothing" / DIY options) and the honest reason a
customer picks you instead.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **Chain café down the street** — cheaper and faster, but generic and burnt-tasting. We win on quality and warmth.
> - **Grocery-store beans** — convenient and cheap, but stale. We win on freshness and story.
> - **Making coffee at home** — free, but rarely as good. We win on the experience and the treat factor.

### Proof points
*Evidence that backs up your claims: testimonials, stats, awards, credentials, notable customers.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - 4.9★ across 400+ Google reviews.
> - "Best Neighborhood Coffee" — Portland Weekly, 2025.
> - Direct-trade relationships with 3 named farms.
> - Baristas complete 40+ hours of training.

### Key messaging pillars
*The 3–4 recurring themes all your marketing should reinforce. Every post, email, and page should
ladder up to one of these.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> 1. **Freshness** — roasted this week, tastes like it.
> 2. **Ethical sourcing** — you know where it came from.
> 3. **Neighborhood warmth** — your third place.
> 4. **Approachable craft** — expertise without the snobbery.

### Marketing channels & content approach
*Where you show up and what kind of content works there. Match this to where your ICP hangs out.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **Instagram** (primary): behind-the-roaster reels, latte art, "what we're loving this week," 4–5×/week.
> - **Email** (owned list): monthly roast updates, subscriber-only bag drops.
> - **Local partnerships**: cross-promo with nearby bookshop and yoga studio.
> - **In-store**: chalkboard specials, a "new this week" bean card, loyalty punch cards.
> - Content principle: teach and invite, don't hard-sell. Show the craft and the people.
