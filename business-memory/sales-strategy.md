# Sales Strategy

### Customer acquisition motion
*How you primarily get customers: inbound (they find you), outbound (you reach out), referral, foot
traffic, partnerships, paid ads, etc. Note the rough mix.*

> *Example (replace me):* Mostly foot traffic and word of mouth (~60%), inbound from Instagram and
> local search (~25%), and outbound B2B for wholesale accounts (~15%). We don't run paid ads yet.

### Pipeline stages
*The steps a customer moves through, from first contact to a closed sale. For each stage, give a short
definition and the "exit criteria" — what has to be true to move to the next stage. Retail businesses
can frame this as the customer journey; B2B as a sales pipeline.*

> *Example (replace me):* **(Wholesale B2B pipeline)**
> 1. **Lead** — a café/restaurant we've identified as a fit. Exit: we have a contact name and email.
> 2. **Contacted** — we've reached out and introduced ourselves. Exit: they reply with interest.
> 3. **Sampling** — we've dropped off free sample beans. Exit: they've tasted and given feedback.
> 4. **Proposal** — we've sent pricing and terms. Exit: they've reviewed and are negotiating.
> 5. **Closed–Won** — signed a standing weekly order. Exit: first delivery scheduled.

### Lead qualification criteria
*The signals that tell you a lead is worth pursuing — your ICP fit filters. What makes a "good" lead?*

> *Example (replace me):* A wholesale lead is qualified if: they're within our 5-mile delivery radius;
> they serve coffee as a real part of their menu (not an afterthought); their brand values quality;
> and they do at least ~20 lbs/month of volume. Below that, it's not worth our roasting time.

### Lead enrichment needs
*What information you want to have on a lead, and WHY each piece matters. This tells Claude what to
research or ask for when helping you work a lead.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **Decision-maker name & role** — so we talk to the person who can say yes, not a server.
> - **Current coffee supplier** — so we know who we're displacing and how to differentiate.
> - **Monthly volume estimate** — so we can qualify and price.
> - **Their brand/values** — so we can tailor the pitch (a farm-to-table spot cares about sourcing).
> - **Recent news** (new location, menu change) — a natural reason to reach out now.

### Outreach approach & cadence
*How you reach out and how persistent you are. Tone, sequence, and timing.*

> *Example (replace me):* Warm and low-pressure — we lead with a free sample, not a pitch. Sequence:
> intro email → drop off samples in person within a week → follow-up 3 days later → final check-in
> after 1 week. If no interest after that, we stop and revisit in 6 months.

### Objection handling playbook
*The objections you hear most, and your honest, on-brand response to each.*

> *Example (replace me):*
> - **"You're more expensive than our current supplier."** → "You are — because it's fresher and
>   direct-trade. Most places find they use less because customers taste the difference. Try it free first."
> - **"We're happy with who we have."** → "Totally fair. Keep our sample around — if anything changes,
>   or you want a local backup, we're right here."
> - **"We don't have time to switch."** → "We handle delivery and setup. It's about 15 minutes of your time."

### Pricing & negotiation guardrails
*Your limits: standard pricing, how much you'll flex, and where you won't. Keeps discounting disciplined.*

> *Example (replace me):* Standard wholesale is $12/lb. We'll go to $11/lb for orders over 40 lbs/month
> on a 6-month commitment. We do not go below $11 — below that we lose money. No free delivery under 20 lbs.

### Handoff & follow-up expectations
*What happens after a sale: onboarding, follow-up, and how you keep the customer.*

> *Example (replace me):* On close, we schedule the first delivery and give the café a "how to store &
> brew our beans" one-pager plus a free staff tasting. We check in at 2 weeks and then monthly. Goal is
> a standing order they never have to think about.
