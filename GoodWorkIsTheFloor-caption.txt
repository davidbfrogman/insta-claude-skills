Your raise was decided months ago, in a room you weren't in.

I've managed teams at Fortune 500s and startups. I've sat in those rooms. Nobody in there is arguing about whether you did good work. Everyone already cleared that bar.

Good work is the floor. They argue about five other things — and zero of them is "worked harder than everyone else."

GOODWILL — do people want you, or route around you?
1. Get requested by name outside your team. A peer manager's praise is evidence. Yours is advocacy.
2. Be the connective tissue inside it. Onboard the new hire — you become their default person for 18 months.

TRAJECTORY — am I paying for who you are, or who you're becoming?
3. Solve a problem one level up, out loud. Bring a proposal, not a complaint.
4. Build the skill your execs keep repeating in all-hands — and flag it BEFORE you finish. Same effort, totally different signal.

TRUST — does it come back done, or come back to them?
5. Ship work that needs no second look. Close the loop before you're asked to.
6. Stop pressing the button you were told to press. Kill it. "I killed a task that ate 6 hours a week" = 15% of a person, back, every week.

FLIGHT RISK — do they have to fight for you, or will you stay anyway?
7. Build a public footprint. Not because you're leaving. Because you want to look like someone who could.
8. Take the recruiter call. Stay calibrated. People with real options get fought for.

LEVERAGE — if you walked out tomorrow, does something break?
9. Own a system end to end. Find the thing everyone complains about and nobody owns.
10. Become the source of truth. Document it publicly — you lose the small dependency, gain the large one.

That's the full list. The list is the easy part.
